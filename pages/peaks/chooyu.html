Peak Overview

Cho Oyu — Peak Overview

  • Height: 8,188 m (26,864 ft)
  • Range: Mahālangur Himal
  • Location: Nepal–Tibet Border
  • Coordinates: 28.0942° N, 86.6608° E
  • First Ascent: 19 October 1954
  • First Climbers: Herbert Tichy, Joseph Jöchler & Pasang Dawa Lama

Cho Oyu, the sixth-highest mountain in the world, is considered one of the most accessible and popular 8000ers. Its broad slopes, gentler gradients, and well-established climbing routes make it a favored peak for climbers training for Everest.

Geography Nepal / Tibet

Geography & Location

Cho Oyu sits west of Everest and Lhotse, forming a major part of the Himalayan skyline above the Nangpa La pass — a historic trade route connecting Tibet and the Khumbu region. Its broad, glaciated slopes make it visually less steep than neighboring peaks, yet impressive in scale.

History First Ascent: 1954

Climbing History & Expeditions

Cho Oyu was first climbed in 1954 by an Austrian expedition using the northwest ridge route. Since then, the peak has grown popular for both commercial and independent expeditions due to its comparatively straightforward terrain and favorable acclimatization profile.

Hazards Crevasses • Whiteouts

Challenges & Dangers

While Cho Oyu is considered easier than most 8000ers, the climb still involves high-altitude risks, including hidden crevasses, avalanche zones, and sudden whiteout storms. The final summit plateau can be disorienting in poor visibility.

Culture Khumbu • Nangpa La

Cultural Significance & Conservation

Cho Oyu overlooks the traditional trade route of Nangpa La, historically used by Tibetan traders and Sherpa communities. Today, the region plays an important role in trekking tourism and cultural exchange, while conservation efforts in Sagarmatha National Park aim to protect its delicate high-altitude ecosystems.
