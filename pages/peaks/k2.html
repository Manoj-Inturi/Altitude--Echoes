K2 — Peak Overview

  • Height: 8,611 m (28,251 ft)
  • Range: Karakoram
  • Location: Pakistan–China Border
  • Coordinates: 35.881° N, 76.513° E
  • First Ascent: 31 July 1954
  • First Climbers: Achille Compagnoni & Lino Lacedelli

K2, the second-highest mountain on Earth, is widely considered the most dangerous and technically demanding of all 8000ers. Nicknamed the “Savage Mountain,” it rises sharply from the Karakoram and is infamous for unpredictable storms, steep ridges, and extremely high fatality rates. Unlike Everest, commercial expeditions are few, making K2 one of the purest tests of skill in mountaineering.

Geography Karakoram Region

Geography & Location

K2 stands at the heart of the Karakoram, rising sharply above glacial valleys such as the Godwin-Austen and Baltoro. The peak’s pyramid-shaped structure features steep ridgelines, massive rock walls, and highly exposed couloirs. Extreme winters, powerful winds, and glacial movement shape its dramatic landscape, making the region both beautiful and unforgiving.

History First Ascent: 1954

Climbing History & Expeditions

Early attempts on K2 were marked by tragedy, difficult weather, and long periods of waiting for ideal conditions. The Italian expedition of 1954 achieved the first ascent, opening a legacy of challenging climbs involving the Abruzzi Spur, Cesen Route, Magic Line, and Northern Ridge. K2 remains one of the least climbed 8000ers, with few successful winter expeditions and a reputation for technical excellence.

Hazards The Bottleneck • Storms

Challenges & Dangers

K2’s most notorious danger is the Bottleneck, a steep couloir beneath unstable seracs that collapse without warning. The mountain’s storms are legendary, trapping climbers for days. Technical rock sections, exposure, avalanches, and extreme cold create some of the harshest climbing conditions on the planet. Fatality rates historically rank among the highest in high-altitude mountaineering.

Culture Gilgit-Baltistan • Karakoram

Culture, Conservation & Tourism

K2 holds deep significance for communities in Gilgit-Baltistan, whose livelihoods are linked to trekking, portering, and expedition support. Unlike Everest, tourism is less commercialized, preserving the raw wilderness of the Karakoram. Environmental challenges include glacier retreat, waste management on the Baltoro Trek, and the need for sustainable climbing practices to protect this remote region.
