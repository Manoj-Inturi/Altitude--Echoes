Lhotse — Peak Overview

  • Height: 8,516 m (27,940 ft)
  • Range: Mahālangur Himal
  • Location: Nepal–Tibet Border
  • Coordinates: 27.9617° N, 86.9330° E
  • First Ascent: 18 May 1956
  • First Climbers: Fritz Luchsinger & Ernst Reiss

Lhotse, the fourth-highest mountain in the world, is closely connected to Everest, sharing the same climbing route through the Khumbu Icefall and Western Cwm. Despite this, it has its own dramatic south face — one of the steepest and most formidable rock walls on Earth.

Geography Nepal / Tibet

Geography & Location

Lhotse consists of three distinct summits: Lhotse Main, Lhotse Middle, and Lhotse Shar. The mountain rises dramatically above the Western Cwm and shares the South Col with Everest. Its geography is dominated by steep ridges, deep glacial valleys, and towering rock faces.

History First Ascent: 1956

Climbing History & Expeditions

Lhotse was first summited in 1956 by a Swiss team as part of a broader Himalayan expedition. Its iconic south face has drawn elite climbers for decades and is considered one of the most difficult walls on the planet. While the normal route shares sections with Everest, Lhotse’s upper couloir creates unique challenges.

Hazards Couloirs • Rockfall

Challenges & Dangers

The Lhotse Face is a steep glacier wall prone to rockfall and icefall, making it one of the most technically challenging sections on the route. High winds, exposure, and crevasses add to the danger. The final couloir to the summit is narrow, icy, and extremely steep.

Culture Khumbu Region

Cultural Significance & Conservation

Lhotse lies within the Sagarmatha National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Sherpa communities living in the Khumbu Valley maintain deep spiritual and cultural ties to the mountain environment. Conservation efforts focus on protecting glaciers, wildlife, and traditional livelihoods.
