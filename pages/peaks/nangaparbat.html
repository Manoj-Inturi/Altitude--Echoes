Nanga Parbat
Peak Overview

Nanga Parbat — Peak Overview

  • Height: 8,126 m (26,660 ft)
  • Range: Himalaya (Western)
  • Location: Pakistan
  • Coordinates: 35.2372° N, 74.5892° E
  • First Ascent: 3 July 1953
  • First Climbers: Hermann Buhl (solo, no oxygen)

Nanga Parbat, the “Killer Mountain,” is one of the most dramatic and dangerous peaks on Earth. Its immense Rupal Face, rising over 4,600 m from base to summit, is the highest mountain wall in the world. Despite its beauty, Nanga Parbat demands respect and meticulous preparation.

Geography Pakistan

Geography & Location

Located at the western end of the Himalaya, Nanga Parbat rises above the Indus River valley in northern Pakistan. Its three main faces — Rupal, Diamer, and Rakhiot — form one of the most complex and varied mountain structures in the region.

History First Ascent: 1953

Climbing History & Expeditions

Early German expeditions in the 1930s suffered devastating losses, giving the mountain its ominous nickname. Nanga Parbat remained unconquered until Hermann Buhl’s remarkable 1953 ascent — a solo summit push without supplemental oxygen, considered one of the greatest feats in mountaineering.

Hazards Avalanches • Extreme Cliffs

Challenges & Dangers

Nanga Parbat’s precipitous walls, severe weather, and avalanche corridors make it one of the deadliest eight-thousanders. The Rupal Face is especially notorious, with complex ice towers and unstable snowfields posing constant threats.

Culture Gilgit-Baltistan

Cultural Significance & Conservation

The region surrounding Nanga Parbat is home to diverse ethnic communities, including the Shina-speaking people of Diamer. The mountain is deeply tied to local folklore and stands as a symbol of natural power and mystery in northern Pakistan.
