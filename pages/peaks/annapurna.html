Peak Overview

Annapurna I — Peak Overview

  • Height: 8,091 m (26,545 ft)
  • Range: Annapurna Massif
  • Location: Nepal
  • Coordinates: 28.5950° N, 83.8203° E
  • First Ascent: 3 June 1950
  • First Climbers: Maurice Herzog & Louis Lachenal (France)

Annapurna I, the tenth-highest mountain in the world, is one of the most dangerous and technically challenging 8000ers. Known for its unpredictable avalanches, steep faces, and complex glacier systems, Annapurna holds a legendary status in the world of mountaineering.

Geography Nepal

Geography & Location

Annapurna rises above the fertile valleys of central Nepal, surrounded by dramatic ridgelines, deep gorges, and massive glacial bowls. The south face is one of the most iconic mountain walls on Earth — a 3,000 m vertical rise of rock, ice, and avalanche-prone terrain.

History First Ascent: 1950

Climbing History & Expeditions

Annapurna I became the first 8000er ever climbed when Maurice Herzog and Louis Lachenal reached its summit in 1950. Their descent, marked by storm, frostbite, and tragedy, is considered one of the most harrowing survival stories in mountaineering history.

Hazards Avalanches • Seracs

Challenges & Dangers

Annapurna is known for its extremely unstable snowpack and some of the highest avalanche fatality rates among 8000ers. The south face especially is a deadly mix of ice cliffs, seracs, and hanging snowfields that collapse unpredictably.

Culture Gandaki Region

Cultural Significance & Conservation

Annapurna holds deep cultural and spiritual meaning for local communities. The surrounding region includes ancient trading villages, Tibetan Buddhist culture, and part of the world-famous Annapurna Conservation Area — Nepal’s largest protected region.
