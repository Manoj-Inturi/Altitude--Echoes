Peak Overview

Kangchenjunga — Peak Overview

  • Height: 8,586 m (28,169 ft)
  • Range: Kangchenjunga Himal
  • Location: Nepal–India Border
  • Coordinates: 27.7025° N, 88.1475° E
  • First Ascent: 25 May 1955
  • First Climbers: Joe Brown & George Band

Kangchenjunga, the third-highest mountain on Earth, is revered for its dramatic ridges, massive glaciated faces, and deep cultural significance. Unlike Everest and K2, it remains far less commercialized, preserving a sense of mystery and raw Himalayan beauty.

Geography Nepal / India

Geography & Location

Kangchenjunga is part of the Kangchenjunga Himal, a subrange of the greater Himalayas. Its massive structure includes five peaks, surrounded by huge glaciers such as Zemu, Yalung, Talung, and Kanchenjunga Glacier, which form critical water sources for regions in Nepal and India.

History First Ascent: 1955

Climbing History & Expeditions

Early attempts in the early 20th century focused on reconnaissance from the Indian side. The successful 1955 British expedition, led by Charles Evans, brought Joe Brown and George Band to the summit — stopping a few feet short to honor local traditions. The mountain remains known for technical difficulty, unpredictable weather, and fewer successful expeditions compared to other 8000ers.

Hazards Avalanches • Seracs

Challenges & Dangers

Kangchenjunga is notorious for unstable snowfields, avalanche-prone slopes, and highly unpredictable storms. Deep crevasses, collapsing seracs, and steep technical sections make it one of the most dangerous 8000ers, with a historically high fatality ratio.

Culture Sikkim • Taplejung

Cultural Significance & Conservation

Kangchenjunga holds deep spiritual importance in both Nepal and India. In Sikkimese culture, it is revered as a sacred mountain, and climbers traditionally stop short of the true summit. Conservation efforts in the Kangchenjunga Conservation Area and India’s Khangchendzonga National Park aim to protect biodiversity, local communities, and fragile mountain ecosystems.
