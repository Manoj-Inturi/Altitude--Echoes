Peak Overview

Manaslu — Peak Overview

  • Height: 8,163 m (26,781 ft)
  • Range: Mansiri Himal
  • Location: Nepal
  • Coordinates: 28.5497° N, 84.5597° E
  • First Ascent: 9 May 1956
  • First Climbers: Toshio Imanishi & Gyalzen Norbu (Japan)

Manaslu, the eighth-highest mountain in the world, is known for its sweeping ridges and striking summit dome. Less commercialized than other peaks, Manaslu offers a classic Himalayan climbing experience and is often considered one of the more accessible 8000ers.

Geography Nepal

Geography & Location

Manaslu rises above the Budhi Gandaki Valley, surrounded by steep ridges, glaciers, and deep ravines. The mountain’s varied topography includes long knife-edged ridgelines and broad snowfields that sweep up toward its massive summit dome.

History First Ascent: 1956

Climbing History & Expeditions

After several reconnaissance missions in the early 1950s, Manaslu was first successfully climbed in 1956 by a Japanese expedition. Japanese climbers maintain a special connection to the peak, often calling it their “sacred mountain.” Popular climbing routes today follow the northeast face.

Hazards Avalanches • Crevasses

Challenges & Dangers

Manaslu poses high risks from serac collapses, deep crevasses, and avalanche-prone slopes, especially between Camps 1 and 2. Changing weather patterns and heavy snowfall frequently complicate climbing conditions during peak season.

Culture Manaslu Region

Cultural Significance & Conservation

The Manaslu region is rich in Tibetan Buddhist culture, with monasteries, prayer flags, and traditional villages dotting the trekking route. Conservation efforts in the Manaslu Conservation Area aim to protect local wildlife, forests, and centuries-old cultural landscapes.
