Peak Overview

Makalu — Peak Overview

  • Height: 8,485 m (27,838 ft)
  • Range: Mahālangur Himal
  • Location: Nepal–Tibet Border
  • Coordinates: 27.8850° N, 87.0883° E
  • First Ascent: 15 May 1955
  • First Climbers: Lionel Terray & Jean Couzy

Makalu, the fifth-highest mountain in the world, is known for its distinctive pyramid shape and steep, knife-edged ridges. More remote and isolated than Everest or Lhotse, Makalu offers a challenging climb requiring technical ice and rock skills.

Geography Nepal / Tibet

Geography & Location

Makalu rises sharply as a four-sided pyramid of rock and ice, surrounded by deep valleys, glaciers, and steep ridges. The Barun Valley to the south is one of the most remote and dramatic landscapes of the eastern Himalaya, home to rare wildlife and lush alpine flora.

History First Ascent: 1955

Climbing History & Expeditions

Makalu was first summited by a French expedition in 1955. Its steep, exposed northeast ridge and technical climbing sections have made it a favorite for strong alpinists. Unlike Everest, Makalu sees far fewer climbers and remains a demanding, less commercialized ascent.

Hazards Steep Ridges • Extreme Weather

Challenges & Dangers

Makalu’s upper pyramid is extremely steep and exposed, requiring technical mixed climbing. Sudden storms, avalanches, and harsh winds are common due to its isolated position in the eastern Himalaya. The final summit push is considered one of the hardest among the 8000ers.

Culture Makalu-Barun Region

Cultural Significance & Conservation

Makalu lies within the Makalu-Barun National Park, one of the most biologically diverse areas in Nepal. Local communities depend on agriculture, herding, and trekking tourism, while conservation initiatives focus on preserving endangered wildlife and protecting fragile terrain.
