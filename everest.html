Mount Everest — Peak Overview

  • Height: 8,848.86 m (29,031 ft)
  • Range: Mahālangur Himal
  • Location: Nepal–Tibet (China) Border
  • Coordinates: 27.9881° N, 86.9250° E
  • First Ascent: 29 May 1953
  • First Climbers: Edmund Hillary & Tenzing Norgay

Everest, the tallest mountain on Earth, stands as a monumental icon of geography, exploration, and human ambition. Rising along the Nepal–Tibet border, the peak has shaped generations of climbers and communities.

Geography Nepal / Tibet

Geography & Location

Mount Everest stands on the crest of the Mahālangur Himal along the Nepal–Tibet border and is shaped by tectonic uplift, intense glaciation, and extreme weather. Its steep ridgelines, sweeping glaciers, and shifting icefalls reflect millions of years of geological activity. The Khumbu Glacier and Rongbuk system feed major Himalayan rivers, influencing regional water supplies. Jet-stream winds, monsoon cycles, and microclimates make Everest's weather highly unpredictable.

History First Ascent: 1953

Climbing History & Expeditions

Everest’s climbing story began with early 20th-century British reconnaissance, advancing toward the landmark 1953 ascent by Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. Over the years, technological improvements, oxygen innovations, refined gear, and advanced forecasting reshaped high-altitude climbing. Commercial expeditions increased accessibility but also raised ethical and safety concerns. New routes, winter climbs, speed records, and oxygen-free ascents continue to define Everest’s evolving mountaineering legacy.

Hazards Death Zone • Icefall

Challenges & Dangers

Everest’s dangers arise from altitude, volatile storms, and complex terrain. Above 8,000m in the death zone, the human body deteriorates rapidly. The Khumbu Icefall shifts constantly, with serac collapses and hidden crevasses. Jet-stream winds and whiteouts appear suddenly. Overcrowding, exhaustion, altitude sickness, and limited rescue capability amplify risk. Everest demands extreme caution, strong judgment, and deep respect for nature’s limits.

Culture Sherpas • Sagarmatha

Culture, Conservation & Tourism

Everest holds deep cultural and spiritual meaning for Himalayan communities. Known as Sagarmatha in Nepal and Chomolungma in Tibet, it is honored through rituals like the puja ceremony. Tourism supports local economies but stresses fragile ecosystems, prompting conservation efforts and cleanup campaigns in Sagarmatha National Park. Sherpa safety, fair labor practices, and climate-driven glacial changes remain central issues for sustaining Everest’s environment and communities.
