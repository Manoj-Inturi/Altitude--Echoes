Dhaulagiri — Peak Overview

  • Height: 8,167 m (26,795 ft)
  • Range: Dhaulagiri Himal
  • Location: Nepal
  • Coordinates: 28.6967° N, 83.4878° E
  • First Ascent: 13 May 1960
  • First Climbers: Kurt Diemberger, Peter Diener, Ernst Forrer, Albin Schelbert

Dhaulagiri, the seventh-highest mountain in the world, dominates the western Nepal skyline with its massive, isolated peak. Known for its steep slopes and dramatic relief, the mountain rises abruptly above the Kali Gandaki Gorge — one of the deepest in the world.

Geography Nepal

Geography & Location

Dhaulagiri’s striking topography includes sheer icefalls, steep ridges, and massive glacial basins. The peak sits west of the Annapurna massif, separated by the world-famous Kali Gandaki Gorge, creating dramatic vertical relief unmatched by most Himalayan peaks.

History First Ascent: 1960

Climbing History & Expeditions

After numerous failed attempts in the 1950s, a Swiss-Austrian expedition successfully summited Dhaulagiri in 1960 using the northeast ridge. Since then, climbers have explored several additional routes, though the mountain remains a serious challenge due to its technical terrain and volatile weather.

Hazards Avalanches • Icefall

Challenges & Dangers

Dhaulagiri’s slopes are known for heavy snow accumulation, frequent avalanches, and complex serac formations. The mountain’s large glaciated sections make it especially hazardous during warm or unstable weather periods.

Culture Western Nepal

Cultural Significance & Conservation

Dhaulagiri is culturally significant to local communities who live along the trekking routes of the Dhaulagiri Circuit. Conservation efforts in the region focus on supporting traditional livelihoods, preserving biodiversity, and managing the environmental impact of trekking and mountaineering activities.
