Mount Elbrus — Peak Overview

  • Height: 5,642 m (18,510 ft)
  • Range: Caucasus Mountains
  • Location: Russia
  • Coordinates: 43.3499° N, 42.4453° E
  • First Ascent: 1829
  • First Climbers: Khillar Khachirov & Russian Expedition

Mount Elbrus is a dormant double-cone stratovolcano and the highest mountain in Europe. Despite being non-technical on the standard route, Elbrus is known for brutal winds, whiteouts, and high altitude that challenge climbers every year.

Geography Russia

Geography & Location

Elbrus sits in the western Caucasus near the border with Georgia. Its twin volcanic domes rise above glaciated slopes, with the west summit being the true highest point. The mountain is surrounded by deep valleys and alpine terrain shaped by millennia of glaciation.

History First Ascent: 1829

Climbing History & Expeditions

The first ascent was achieved in 1829 during a Russian scientific expedition. Mount Elbrus later became a major mountaineering objective and a symbol of Soviet-era alpine achievement, leading to hundreds of ascents every year.

Hazards Cold • Whiteouts

Challenges & Dangers

Elbrus is notorious for rapid weather shifts, freezing winds, whiteouts, crevasses, and the long summit day that pushes climbers through extreme exposure. Many accidents occur due to disorientation in sudden storms.

Culture Caucasus Region

Cultural Significance & Conservation

Elbrus has cultural significance in local Kabardian and Balkar traditions, often appearing in regional folklore as a sacred peak. The mountain lies within a protected national park, preserving wildlife, glaciers, and high-altitude ecosystems.
